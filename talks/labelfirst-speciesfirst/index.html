Appendix: the effect is causal, not a backbone coincidence

Could the benefit just be luck in which AI model we picked? We tested it the hard way.
  • Real models fall into two camps (CLIP vs DINOv3), so a single trend line across them would overstate a “law.” We say so.
  • So we ran a controlled sweep: dial species from blurred-together to cleanly-separated on purpose, and watch the benefit. It rises smoothly — Pearson 0.997. The geometry is doing the work.
  • Confirmed 4 independent ways: real models, this synthetic sweep, predicting held-out models (R² 0.61), and re-labelling crews (agree within 1–2 pt).

Appendix: how we pick, and the pre-flight

One number, computed before you label anything, predicts whether smart picking will beat random.
  • Picking the next photo: 16 interchangeable strategies behind one call. The default, CoreSet, simply spreads picks to cover the space — no tuning, an unmodified 2018 method. (A co-pick, novel_margin, leans toward brand-new species when you expect them.)
  • The pre-flight — separability: how cleanly the model already sorts species apart, scored without a single label.
  • We proved it's the geometry, not a fluke: keep the photos and labels fixed, just blur the model's view — the benefit falls in lockstep (correlation 0.76 BCI, 0.94 PlantNet, 0.96 iNat).
  • Necessary, not magic: one “cleanup” step kept separability flat yet still cost ~16 points — because it quietly tangled the space behind the score.

Appendix: we couldn't move the goalposts

We fixed the pass/fail test before seeing any result — so we couldn't fool ourselves into a win.
  • The rule, locked in advance: a standard significance test on per-run scores, plus a 10,000-resample confidence interval that must exclude zero, corrected for testing many things at once.
  • Why 8 runs: with all 8 pointing the same way, the math can't score better than 1-in-256 (p ≈ 0.004) — enough margin to survive the correction; 5 runs wouldn't. We got all 8 positive, on 3 different models.
  • The mistake we keep on the slide: an early “our picker trains a better model” win was really a 25–29% data leak — the picker had peeked at the test crowns. Re-run honestly: no picker dominates. We added a tripwire that fails the build if a held-out crown ever reaches a picker again.

Appendix: what didn't work, and why that matters

We show the failures on purpose — they're the evidence we're not overselling.
  • Picking the hardest-to-label photos lost every run (0/16). Hard to label ≠ rare species.
  • “Cleaning up” the embedding first backfired — it erased the very structure the picker relies on.
  • Ranking models by a quick accuracy proxy didn't predict discovery. Measure discovery directly.

So the contribution isn't a clever new algorithm — it's the honest, falsifiable test harness. An unmodified 2018 method plus that protocol beats the industrial half-random hybrid and plain random.

We also reproduce the Beery transductive-needles method exactly (Kurinchi-Vendhan & Beery 2026, arXiv:2606.03821; per-point correlation 1.000).
