The Geometry of Discovery

A label-free pre-flight for finding unknown species
Wietze Suijker
Species-discovery PhD · the spine paper (Ch. 2)

Most species are unnamed. Most known ones are barely mapped.

The bottleneck isn't sensors or models. It's human attention: which specimen a taxonomist examines next, and where a naturalist goes next.
The question: can we predict, before spending a single label or field trip, where that attention will pay off? A cheap, a-priori “pre-flight” for discovery.

First, test the simplest hypothesis

Hypothesis: a single statistic (effective dimension) governs both “which to label” (feature space) and “where to sample” (geographic space). Test: the same estimator on both axes, 30 taxonomic orders. Result: no association — Spearman −0.06, 95% CI [−0.14, +0.06], includes zero. The hypothesis is rejected; the strong coupling at a few coarse “iconic” taxa was an aggregation artifact. So the two axes are independent, and the pre-flight must be two-dimensional.

So what does govern discovery? The gaps, not the cloud.

Picture each species as a cloud of points. If the clouds sit apart with clear gaps, an unknown species lands in a gap and stands out. The inter-species margin governs both discovery tools (+0.65 / +0.63); how high-dimensional the data is (the one-number bet) governs neither (+0.22 / +0.02).

The detector is the geometry

A closed-form cluster separability predicts the actual open-set detector's per-clade accuracy at Pearson 0.91 (mean error 0.04), with no detector run and under a leave-one-species-out recompute. The score isn't correlated with the detector; it essentially is the detector.

Within one dataset, margin and the detector read the same geometry, so that agreement alone would be near-circular. The real evidence: margin measured on five continents predicts the unseen sixth — different data, so it cannot be circular.

Both discovery tools work on every continent, untuned

External validation on independent iNaturalist pulls from all six terrestrial realms: the open-set detector spots new species at AUROC 0.64–0.68, and novelty-guided selection finds species +16–24% faster than random — no tuning.

It's a property of the representation, not the benchmark

The margin predicts discoverability at every zoom (order +0.65, family +0.61, genus +0.62), under a different backbone (DINOv2 +0.76), and in other realms (+0.52, +0.57) — while the one-number bet goes flat or negative. The same separability law even appears in birdsong audio.

And it forecasts genuinely region-novel species

The detector holds from random held-out species (0.69) through the data-poorest (0.66) to genuinely region-novel species new to a continent (0.69). The margin forecasts which clades hide them at +0.56, where the one-number bet gives ~0.

Operationally: run an unsupervised first pass over a whole region for free, then spend labels only where the rough signal says discovery will pay off.

What I refuse to claim

  • It's a deployment-condition diagnostic, not a model selector. Across distinct backbones, margin and accuracy move together — the signal is real across datasets and difficulty, not across models.
  • Whole-genus-held-out detection is weaker (AUROC 0.58 vs 0.65): finding a truly new lineage is harder than a new species with known cousins. That gap is what the field trial tests.
  • Leakage checked and closed: on truly-unseen species the detector scores 0.69–0.76 — at or above the random-novel baseline, so the headline isn't inflated.

Why this is the thesis spine

  • No prior work connects a label-free separability statistic to open-set discovery benefit before any model is trained or any budget spent.
  • The empirical law is the floor; the next rung is a bound — under a margin / low-noise condition, the inter-species margin lower-bounds the expected information gain of a query.
  • Two corollaries inherit its novelty: the open-set discovery loop (P2) and the geographic value-of-information dual (P3).
Predict where discovery pays off, before you pay for it.

Appendix: two axes, two specialists

The refuted bet, made useful.
  • Feature-space novelty wins for species discovery (+20% over random); spatial coverage barely moves it (+8%).
  • Spatial coverage wins for geographic cell coverage (+77%); novelty barely moves it (+1%).
  • Different signal wins in each space — that's why one number can't span both, and why the pre-flight is irreducibly two-dimensional.
