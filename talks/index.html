Talks

A PhD on efficient species discovery, told as the individual papers it breaks into. Finding which species exist and where they are is one value-of-information problem, governed by a label-free “pre-flight” you can compute before spending a label or a field trip.

Narrative order below is the spine first. The publication order is different: “Novelty is not Rarity” is the most scoop-exposed unit and ships first; the “Geometry of Discovery” spine is the defensible long game. Every number in every deck traces to a committed pilot run.

The three papers
Chapter 2 · the spine

The Geometry of Discovery

A label-free pre-flight — the inter-species margin — predicts where discovery pays off, before you pay for it. The seductive “one number unifies everything” bet, refuted by my own data.

margin governs both discovery tools · transfers to unseen continents (+0.59) · backbone- and resolution-invariant
Chapter 3 · the Linnean shortfall

Novelty is not Rarity

Under a tiny annotation budget, label-free novelty is the best proxy for the rarity you can't yet measure — and a two-pronged pre-flight covers where it fails. The causal test is a queue we control.

open-set AUROC 0.66 · +21% faster discovery · queue RCT as the causal anchor
Chapter 4 · the Wallacean shortfall

Where to look next

Where to sample is a value-of-information question — answerable, leakage-free, and genuinely separate from what to label. The honest negative: spatial coverage, not embedding novelty, wins here.

ρ 0.49–0.64 backtest · the two axes are decoupled · joint VOI +21.7%
