Novelty is not Rarity

Open-set discovery of tropical tree species under a tiny budget
Wietze Suijker
active learning · tropical tree crowns

The budget problem

A botanist can label a few hundred drone-imaged tree crowns. Which ones should they see, to discover the most new species per hour?
The catch: you want the rare species, but rarity is a label you don't have until after you've labelled. So you need a proxy you can compute on raw, unlabelled crowns. Novelty — distance in embedding space — is the best obtainable one.

The label-free margin forecasts which unseen species are detectable

For 210 species the model never saw — including 80 whole-genus- unseen new lineages — the label-free margin predicts how detectable each one is at Spearman +0.66. No labels, no detector run: just the distance to the nearest known species.

A parameter-free open-set detector that works

Spotting a new species is feasible above chance in all 30 clades (mean AUROC 0.66), untuned. How high-dimensional the data is barely predicts which clades are easy — which is exactly why the pre-flight is the margin, not the dimension.

Choosing distinctive examples beats random, and the limit is honest

The same selection principle, validated across 30 taxonomic orders: choosing the most distinctive examples to label next finds +21% more species than random (beating random in 93% of them), while spread-out geographic selection barely helps. Honest limit: the pre-flight only weakly forecasts which taxa will benefit most.

The recipe: don't label what the model already knows

End to end on real BCI crowns: moving from a naive general-backbone reading to the optimal (a domain embedding + whitening) doubles the detector's skill above chance (0.642 → 0.785). Conformal tiering also parks the crowns the model is sure about, so the botanist's hours go to the uncertain tail.

The pre-flight has two prongs, because it fails in two ways

Prong 1 — margin (proactive): on real BCI crowns, across five backbones, the margin predicts per-family detection (the scatter above) — select where species separate cleanly. Prong 2 — where those gaps compress: congeneric species overlap, the model confidently mislabels the rarer twin as its common cousin, and margin can't flag it — so a crown-map disagreement channel routes every such crown to a botanist.

From correlation to cause: a trial we control

  • The causal test lives here, not on the geographic axis: a pre-registered randomization of the botanist queue — margin/novelty-prioritized vs random — scored on new-species IDs per botanist-hour.
  • Because the queue is the deployment we operate, the labeller acts on exactly what we serve: no volunteer-compliance gap, no third-party infrastructure.
  • Powered by simulation on data in hand; the de-risked way to turn the correlation into cause. (Designed and powered — not yet run.)

What I refuse to claim

  • Leakage checked and closed: on truly-unseen species the detector scores 0.69–0.76 — at or above the random-novel baseline, so the headline AUROC isn't inflated by names the backbone already saw.
  • Whole-genus-held-out is weaker (0.58 vs 0.65): detecting a genuinely new lineage is harder than a new species with known cousins. That residual gap is what the field trial measures.
  • This is selection on top of an existing backbone, benchmarked against Open-Insect protocols on SelvaBox / CanopyRS crowns — the contribution is the pre-flight and the loop, not a new model.

Thank you

Under a budget, label-free novelty is the best proxy for the rarity you can't yet measure — and a two-pronged pre-flight covers where it fails.

Appendix: difficulty travels with the clade, not the map

You can budget before the first crown is labelled.
  • Some clades are easy to discover in (visually distinctive); some are hard (visually samey).
  • Leave-one-continent-out: a taxon-aware pre-flight forecasts an unseen continent's per-clade difficulty at +0.57, versus +0.19 for the one-number geometry.
  • So difficulty is predictable in advance — allocate the budget where discovery will pay off.
