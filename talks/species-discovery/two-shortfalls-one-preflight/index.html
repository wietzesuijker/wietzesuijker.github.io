Two Shortfalls, One Pre-flight

Efficient species discovery as value of information
Wietze Suijker
the big picture · two spaces, one pre-flight

Two ways we are blind to biodiversity

Linnean shortfall (what exists)

  • most species are still unnamed
  • which observation should a taxonomist label next?

Wallacean shortfall (where they are)

  • most known species are barely mapped
  • where should a naturalist go observe next?

One problem, not two: value of information

Both are the same question — where will the next unit of human attention pay off most? — posed in two spaces: the feature space of foundation-model embeddings (which to label) and the geographic space of sampling effort (where to go).
Each space carries a label-free pre-flight: a cheap, a-priori test of when targeting beats random — computable on raw, unlabelled data because embeddings give a geometry without labels.

The pilot arc, in one figure

One statistic for both spaces is rejected (A); the two discovery tools still generalize across six realms (B); and difficulty is forecastable on a continent you've never sampled — +0.57 vs +0.19 for the rejected statistic (C).

The mechanism: gaps between species, not cloud size

The pre-flight is the inter-species margin — how clear the gaps between species clusters are. It governs both discovery tools (+0.65 / +0.63); how high-dimensional the data is (the one-number bet) governs neither.

Three results

  • The Geometry of Discovery — the margin pre-flight predicts open-set discovery and transfers to unseen continents (+0.59); the detector is the geometry (Pearson 0.91).
  • Novelty is not Rarity — open-set discovery on tropical crowns under a budget; a two-pronged pre-flight; a botanist-queue trial we control.
  • Where to look next — geographic value-of-information; the honest negative that coverage, not novelty, wins here; the two axes are decoupled.

Why the two axes are separate, and that's the point

Feature-novelty wins for discovering species (+20%); spatial coverage wins for covering places (+77%). Each axis is a specialist — neither can be read off the other, so the pre-flight is irreducibly two-dimensional.

The result survives its own controls

Two controls guard the claim. (1) Falsification: the single-statistic hypothesis is rejected outright — a negative result that establishes the two axes are non-redundant, not a setback. (2) Leakage: the margin–detector link is re-checked for self-membership bias; a leave-one-species-out recompute strengthens it (Pearson 0.91 → 0.93), so the headline isn't inflated by names the geometry already saw.

From correlation to cause: a ladder, not a leap

  • Done: a leakage-free backtest — high-priority cells out-discover low-priority ones at equal effort.
  • Next: a candidate-controlled botanist-queue trial — the labeller acts on exactly what we serve, so there is no compliance gap and no third-party infrastructure.
  • Upside: a randomized bioblitz field trial, once the field logistics and approvals align.
