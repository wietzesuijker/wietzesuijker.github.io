Wietze Suijker · Research brief

A label-free pre-flight for biodiversity discovery

A closed-form embedding-geometry statistic predicts open-set species detectability at Pearson +0.99 on two benchmarks I did not build — net of classifier accuracy, with no detector trained and no label spent.

In plain English

Most species on Earth are still unnamed, and most named ones are barely mapped. The real bottleneck isn't cameras or AI models — it's expert attention: which photo a taxonomist looks at next, and where a naturalist goes next.

The way an AI model arranges species in its memory already tells you, before you spend a single hour of expert time, where looking will pay off. If a group's known species sit cleanly apart in that arrangement, the model will spot new ones there; if they're jumbled, it won't. It's a cheap pre-flight check for discovery.

The idea

The hard limits on biodiversity knowledge are the Linnean shortfall (most species unnamed) and the Wallacean shortfall (most named species barely mapped). Both are, underneath, the same decision problem: which observation should an expert examine next, and where should a naturalist go next. The bottleneck is human attention, not sensors or models.

The geometry of foundation-model embeddings prices that decision in advance. The inter-species margin — how cleanly a group's known species separate in a frozen embedding space — predicts, with no detector trained and no label spent, where discovery effort will pay off. It is a label-free pre-flight: a cheap a-priori estimate of what is worth looking at.

Where it fits

This is the prediction layer that sits underneath open-set insect-detection benchmarks and beside spatial sampling models, squarely inside the mandate of monitoring biodiversity change at scale. Open-Insect measures which novel species a detector catches; BATIS updates a species-distribution model wherever data happens to land. The pre-flight forecasts which groups a detector will catch, and which regions are worth sampling, before the run — turning both benchmarks into label-free planning signals. Crucially it is a meta-predictor, not a rival detector: it forecasts which clades any detector — the standard maximum-softmax-probability baseline included — will find easy, so it complements those benchmarks rather than competing with them. The application makes discovery cheaper and targeted; the core is a transferable result — a geometric predictor with a closed-form mechanism.

Verified · every number traceable to a committed run

What I have shown

  • Detectability is predictable a-priori — off data I did not build. A closed-form binormal cluster-separability is the open-set AUROC (the classical AUROC = Φ(d′/√2) identity fed a frozen-embedding d′): it reproduces the empirical detector at Pearson +0.995 on iNat2021-OSR and +0.997 on TerraIncognita, no detector trained — and it even predicts the two sub-chance clades correctly (Coleoptera d′<0 → AUROC 0.37, empirical 0.38). On a 30-order corpus it holds at +0.91 (MAE 0.04). The contribution is the composition and its empirical validation, not the textbook identity.
  • It predicts clades nobody has sampled. Margin measured on five continents forecasts the unseen sixth's per-order difficulty at Spearman +0.59 (permutation p=2×10⁻⁴, CI over orders [0.37, 0.73]), with +0.35 signal beyond a taxon-identity lookup (p=0.005, positive in all 6 folds).
  • It is not a re-skin of accuracy. The known closed-set↔open-set correlation is exactly the effect I partial out — and signal remains underneath: net of a closed-set-accuracy ceiling, margin holds partial Spearman +0.31 (p=10⁻²¹) and Fisher ratio +0.40 (p=10⁻³⁶) across 934 clade-cells / 359 clades / 10 realm-groups / 3 backbones, surviving family-wise correction — while RankMe, the standard label-free quality score, anti-predicts here (−0.33). The honest drop from raw +0.65 to +0.31 is the point.
  • It survives the leakage test that kills most such claims. Against BioCLIP's literal TreeOfLife-10M training manifest, 116 genuinely out-of-training species are detected at AUROC 0.92, and margin forecasts their per-species detectability at +0.92.
  • It is a property of the representation, not a benchmark. Holds across six realms, order→family→genus, four backbones spanning two pretraining paradigms, and birdsong audio — positive in 12 of 12 slices (sign test p=2×10⁻⁴).
  • On the open-set benchmark, honestly: a parameter-free frozen-BioCLIP detector reproduces Open-Insect's difficulty ordering — local moths hardest, non-local easier, non-moths easiest — monotonically in all three regions (e.g. C-America AUROC 0.91 / 0.95 / 0.99). The fine-grained per-clade prediction needs finer granularity than three regions afford; that is where the 934-cell corpus earns its +0.91.

The integrity, up front

  • I killed my own favourite hypothesis — that a single effective-dimension invariant would unify what to label and where to sample. On real data the two axes correlate at Spearman −0.06 (CI through zero); the strong coupling I first saw was an ecological-fallacy artifact at iconic mega-groups that vanished at order resolution. The decoupling makes the framework stronger: the pre-flight is irreducibly two-dimensional, the two shortfalls genuinely non-redundant.
  • I caught my own leak. A self-membership bug inflated a cross-modal estimator; its faithfulness had collapsed to 0.42 until a leave-one-out fix restored it to 0.94, and I re-verified the pilot headline held under the correction. A standing reasoning-bank of eight such caught traps (single-outlier leverage, pseudoreplicated CIs, a moderator that failed a permutation test) backs every headline.
One question, three depths

What it could become

When, and why, is biological discovery predictable, and can that predictability be exploited before any cost is paid? Framed as value-of-information across the two shortfalls. Honestly, the two legs are not equally proven: the what-to-label (Linnean) leg is nailed (+0.91 on our data, +0.99 off it); the where-to-sample (Wallacean) leg is promising but earlier (+0.59 cross-realm), and is exactly what the field trial below is built to settle.

the law & its limit

The mechanism

The empirical predictor is the result; the next rung is a label-free bound on achievable discovery gain and the regime where targeting cannot help. Lead with the working law; treat the bound as the rigorous follow-on, not the load-bearing claim.

guarantee

Calibrated discovery

A calibrated “this is a new species” under a budget, on the live BCI / CanopyRS loop, benchmarked against the open-set insect benchmark.

causality

The field trial

A pre-registered, candidate-controlled botanist-queue randomized trial on the live BCI loop: does acting on the pre-flight cause more discovery per human-hour? Powered by simulation (~5,600 crowns/arm; +4.9 rare species at a 25% budget). Planned, with no external dependency to start.

Status

The deployment layer and the research are the same object: the continental-scale prediction tooling being built for biodiversity teams runs on exactly the law this brief proves. The rare value is shipping the pipeline and proving the law it runs on — most work does one end or the other and stalls at the seam. The mechanism paper (the +0.99 result above) is drafted; a second paper — the BCI deployment plus the pre-registered field trial — is drafted too.

Positioning

The work this builds on and is distinct from:

  • Open-Insect (NeurIPS 2025 D&B Spotlight; arXiv:2503.01691) — measures novel-species detectability with 38 detectors; this makes it predictable a-priori from geometry.
  • BATIS (AAAI 2026 oral; arXiv:2510.19749) — updates species-distribution models from passive data and names “guide sampling effort” as future work; the Wallacean leg is that guidance, and label-free where BATIS needs predictive uncertainty.
  • Shortfalls framing — the established 2015 formulation, and the Nature Reviews Biodiversity 2025 shortfalls review.
  • Distinct from: the closed-set↔open-set correlation objection (ICLR 2022 — answered net-of-accuracy and with a mechanism, not a correlation); closed-set transferability scores (CVPR 2022 — open-set novelty is not recoverable from them); RankMe (ICML 2023 — anti-predicts here); iterative range active learning (NeurIPS 2023 — this is one-shot, label-free, novel-species). BioCLIP-2 / Perch-2.0 observe separability in species embeddings; this turns it into a predictive value-of-information signal across both shortfalls.
